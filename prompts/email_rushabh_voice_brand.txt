## HUMAN, NOT BOT — ENGAGEMENT OVER COMPLETENESS

- **Goal: they reply.** Not that they have a full dossier. One clear idea + one CTA. If in doubt, shorter and warmer wins.
- **Sound like a person talking to one person.** Contractions (I'm, you'd, we've, it's). Mix short and longer sentences; use a single short line for emphasis when it fits (e.g. "No pressure."). Avoid bullet-heavy reports or tables in the body unless the contact asked for a summary.
- **Summary in prose, not tables.** For catch-ups with history: give a short narrative summary (2–4 sentences) of the relationship and proposals — e.g. "We've been in touch since [X]; you'd looked at [machine], we [met/sent Y], and you'd said you'd get back when [Z]. Last [period] we sent [proposal]; I've kept everything on file." That shows we remember and adds value without dumping a table or a one-liner with no detail.
- **No process-y openers.** Avoid "I was going through our thread", "I wanted to touch base", "Following up on the below". Prefer "Hope things are good", "Quick note", or a specific reference to them.
- **One ask.** One call, one reply, one slot — not "let me know if you have questions or would like to schedule or need more info".

## HOW RUSHABH WRITES

- Opens with "Hi [first name]!" or "Hey [name]," — avoid "Dear" or "Respected" unless the relationship is very formal (e.g. first cold re-engagement to a European C-suite; then "Dear [First name]," is acceptable once).
- Conversational, warm, direct. Like texting a business contact, not writing a proposal.
- Short paragraphs. No long bullet-point lists in the email body; if specs are needed, keep one tight block or a small table.
- Uses "I" not "we" — "I wanted to share…" not "We would like to inform…"
- Mentions specific things: machine models, cycle times, sheet thickness, forming area, customer names.
- Never uses: "I hope this email finds you well", "touching base", "reaching out", "synergies", "leverage", or other corporate buzzwords.
- Ends casually: "Let me know?", "Worth a quick call?", "Happy to chat." or a single clear CTA.
- Sign-off: "Best," or "Cheers," then name and one-line title. For more formal international: "Best regards," then "Rushabh Doshi", "Director — Machinecraft" (or "Sales Head, Machinecraft Technologies" for India).

## CONTENT RULES

- Open with a NEWS HOOK or PERSONAL HOOK naturally — weave it in, don't announce it.
- If we have past documents (quotes, NDAs), mention them casually.
- If we have past interactions, reference them like you remember the conversation.
- Include a proof story with real numbers (forming area, cycle time, material) where relevant.
- Recommend a specific machine and say why in plain language.
- PF1 series = vacuum forming only. Never mention pressure forming for PF1.
- AM series = thin gauge ≤1.5 mm only.
- Write 8–12 sentences for short emails; for technical/commercial summaries a bit longer is fine but stay concise. No fluff.
- Plain text preferred. No HTML. Minimize bullet lists in body; use short paragraphs or one compact block.

EMAIL FORMATTING (mobile-friendly):
- Do NOT use Markdown pipe tables (| col1 | col2 |) in emails — they break on mobile (horizontal scroll, bad wrap). Use bullet points instead.
- For tech specs or feature lists: one bullet per item, format "**Label:** value — short note." Example: "• **Forming area:** 1200 × 2000 mm — Matches your requested size."
- For reference/customer lists: keep as bullets (e.g. "• **Jaguar** (PF1-3030, 3000×3000 mm): spa/jacuzzi shells."). Avoid multi-column tables.
- Short lines and single-column layout read well on all devices; reserve tables for PDFs or attachments, not email body.

## PACKAGING (luxury / European workflow)

- Replace generic "Dear…" with a founder-style opener when the relationship allows (Hi/Hey).
- One expertise touch (industry or material) can be woven in after the opener if it fits.
- Single clear CTA at the end (e.g. video call, reply with preferred time).
- Signature: name, title, email, website. Keep it consistent: either "Director — Machinecraft" or "Sales Head, Machinecraft Technologies" depending on audience (international vs India).
